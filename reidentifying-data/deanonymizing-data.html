Privacy Concerns of the Baltimore Open Data Project

Jillian Luicci, Amanjot Samra, and Chandler Haukap

Introduction

We are a group of student researchers at the University of California, Berkeley advocating for the removal of potentially harmful data and/or datasets from the Open Baltimore data portal and the Baltimore PD social media accounts. One of our team members came across Open Baltimore while examining a slew of public data sets, and we are writing this report to express our concern for the people of Baltimore and the magnitude of risks posed to them by the release of these data sets and information. We will examine the legal and ethical implications associated with the release of these datasets, as well as the risks and some recommendations to remediate said risks.

Overview of the data  

Accountability, accessibility, and transparency are three important aspects when it comes to examining how governments collect and use information. The government of Baltimore hopes to achieve greater levels in each of these aspects in the context of its data collection via its Open Baltimore data inventory, which contains hundreds of public data sets. Furthermore, the city hopes to facilitate the data-driven work requirements of its employees by consolidating data and making it available in one place. The focus of this letter is the data sets related to arrests, 9/11 calls, and the administration of Naloxone for drug overdoses that have been included in this data portal by the government of Baltimore.

According to the Department’s website, the goal of publishing this data is “to promote transparency and data sharing between city government and its citizens”. (Open Baltimore) In Helen Nissenbaum’s work on contextual integrity, Nissenbaum emphasized the importance of understanding the context of datasets(Nissenbum, 2011). Applying this framework, it is our understanding that citizens expect access to this data because it is collected on the back of government work which is funded by citizen tax dollars. Further, we believe that the intention of releasing this data is to allow citizens to analyze the types and locations of various crimes and incidents. This information has the potential to help people make informed decisions regarding where to live or whether additional safety measures are necessary for certain areas.

Nissenbaum's contextualization allows us to conceptualize our privacy boundaries, while Daniel Solove’s privacy taxonomy enables us to identify specific attacks against the Baltimore data. By identifying attacks and proactively preventing them we avoid any privacy harms.

The elements from the taxonomy that we’ve identified as the most vulnerable in Baltimore’s data are (Solov 2006):

  1. Information Processing, in the form of aggregation, identification, insecurity, and exclusion.
  2. Invasion, in the form of decisional inference.

While there are interesting arguments to be made for changes to information collection by the government, it is well outside the power of Open Baltimore to affect this collection. Therefore, we will exclude it in our suggestions. Similarly, by virtue of the public nature of this dataset, we cannot expect to protect the information from dissemination attacks. However, Open Baltimore does have agency to mitigate both information processing and invasion risks.

The dangers posed by information processing have sweeping social consequences. Baltimore’s data contains information on some of the most vulnerable members of society like crime victims and drug addicts. A malicious actor could use relatively straightforward data aggregation to identify individuals and compromise their sense of security. Conversely, there could be exclusionary practices inherent in this data that reduces its efficacy in “opening” Baltimore.

Invasion attacks in the form of decisional inference lead to systemic injustices migrating from the physical world to the digital. What decisions can companies make using this information? Do we as a society accept that this information could be used by employers to make employment decisions? Will franchises avoid building stores in an area that receives too many 911 calls? Baltimore must be conscious of the types of decisions that this information could be used to make.

Next, we will provide some examples of attacks that malicious actors could perform, keeping in mind both Nissenbaum’s contextual privacy and Solove’s taxonomy. We will also suggest changes that minimize our concerns.

Reidentifying overdose victims

Baltimore releases two datasets on the distribution of Naloxone, a nasal spray often sold under the brand name Narcan. One dataset describes the administration of Naloxone by EMTs, and the other documents occurrences of medical personnel leaving Naloxone doses with families that have an at-risk individual living with them.

Naloxone is used to reverse the effects of an opioid overdose. Therefore, the administration or distribution of naloxone implies that an individual is addicted to either heroin or over-the-counter painkillers like oxycodone. These individuals would suffer irreversible reputational damage if the knowledge that they are addicted to opioids were to enter the public domain. Not only that but their families could be damaged as well by the social stigma of living with an addict.

Both naloxone datasets published by the city of Baltimore anonymize the data by including only three pieces of information.

  1. The date of the administration.
  2. The number of doses either administered or distributed.
  3. The zip code of the administration.

Given the size of the city of Baltimore, this information on its own is not enough to identify an individual with reasonable accuracy. However, in our investigation we identified other datasets that could be combined with the naloxone dataset to gain additional information about the victim.

Imagine the following scenario:

  1. A victim overdoses and is found by a family member or friend.
  2. The family member or friend calls 911 to report a narcotics overdose.
  3. EMTs arrive and administer naloxone.
  4. The police arrive and charge the victim with possession of a controlled dangerous substance (CDS).

We identified a way to use this scenario to re-identify the victim.

First, it is possible to join the 911 call log to either of the naloxone datasets using zip code and date. Then, you can narrow the calls by searching for “narcotics” in the call category. This gives you a list of drug-related 911 calls on the same day and in the same zip code as an opioid overdose. While this doesn’t guarantee a match, it does narrow down the search for a malicious actor looking to exploit the opioid epidemic. And, because the 911 call logs contain a street address, we can further identify the victims if they are charged with a crime like possession of a controlled substance.

Another dataset on Open Baltimore contains data on arrests for 6-month periods. The data contains information about the person who is arrested, including age, gender, and race. There are further details regarding the coordinates, address, neighborhood, date, and time of the arrest. Lastly, the data contains details about the charge(s) associated with each arrest.

With the street address and date of a potential opioid overdose, we can join it to the arrest records. Then, we can narrow matches down to arrest for the possession of a controlled dangerous substance (CDS). This would probably prove more fruitful for reidentifying overdose victims in possession of heroin. The arrest records enable us to learn about the age, race, and gender of the individual.

In summary, by performing an aggregation attack, a malicious actor could learn the street address, race, gender, and age of a victim of opioid addiction. This amount of information is more than enough to perform an identification attack to learn the name of the victim using their social media or by questioning their neighbors. Once de-anonymized, the information could be subject to a secondary use attack. Perhaps an insurance provider uses a decisional inference attack to deny the victim healthcare. Perhaps a drug cartel uses it to target customers.

Hopefully, this dystopian scenario is enough to convince Open Baltimore that they need to secure the privacy of their citizens. To identify how Open Baltimore can go about this, we would first like to explain our understanding of the context in which this data is presented. Opioid addiction is recognized by the federal government as an epidemic (Department of Health and Human Services 2021) and as an epidemic, the naloxone datasets are published with the intent to track its spread. We therefore propose the following hierarchy to the criticality of granular data:

  1. Location
  2. Date
  3. Doses

We don’t want to sacrifice any more precision of the location data because it is critical in tracking. We can, however, reduce a little precision in the date. If the data were aggregated to the weekly level, it could still be used for tracking the epidemic over a monthly or yearly scale while simultaneously increasing the difficulty of an attacker sevenfold.

Reidentifying murderers and their victims

On the Baltimore Police Department website (https://www.baltimorepolice.org/), there is a ‘Follow Us’ Section with links to the Department’s various social media profiles. The Baltimore City Police Department actively uses these channels to connect with the community. These pages contain information about active investigations, arrests, and community events. At the time this letter was written, the Facebook page had 232k followers, the Twitter account had 295k followers, and the Instagram account had 20k followers.

On the aforementioned social media pages, the posts about murder arrests in particular are very detailed. The posts contain information on the suspect’s name, age, arrest charges, and even a photograph. Using this text information and inferring the suspect’s gender and race from the photograph, we can narrow the set of suspects in the Baltimore arrest dataset. Further, we can narrow it by the date range within a week of the Facebook post. Using this methodology, there were multiple arrests contained in the dataset for which we were able to identify the murder suspect. With this, we also become aware of some personal information about the victim(s) and details of the victims. The social media posts state the date and time of the crime, the location of the crime, the age of the victim, and the injuries of the victim.

Reidentifying suspects and victims of murders can have serious repercussions. First, every adult in the United States has the right to due process. This means that suspects should be considered innocent until proven guilty. By releasing murder arrests on social media, the Police often strip suspects of this right. Briefly looking at the comments section of the posts, you’ll see that the community often writes hateful comments about the individuals under the assumption that they are already guilty. Posting to social media amplifies unequal treatment by allowing people to take part in the conversation regarding serious legal matters. What if the suspect is deemed innocent? Does Baltimore post any type of correction to the dataset and social media? If not, the suspect has incurred unjust slander. Additionally, the victims and their families could be reidentified based on the data. This could cause unnecessary pain and suffering to the families of the victims. The suspects, victims, and victims’ family members did not consent to their information being publicly disseminated to citizens. Lastly, there are risks associated with secondary use of this data. With victims identified, funeral homes and attorneys could send targeted advertisements to the families of the victims. Additionally, the media could target the families and the communities of the victim for interviews and comments. Similarly, attorneys and the media could target the reidentified murder suspects.

We suggest revisiting Nissenbaum's contextual integrity and making some changes to better align with the data definitions. Additionally, the Fair Information Practice Principles (“FIPPs”) are a set of guidelines to assist with privacy challenges associated with Personally Identifiable Information (“PII”). Per FIPPs, there should be specific purpose specifications of each of the datasets(The Fair Information). For the arrest dataset, the context of the data seemingly intends to allow the public to understand crime concentration in Baltimore by types of crimes and neighborhoods. In this context, the data could be aggregated and presented in a dashboard. The dashboard would be a user-friendly mechanism to share the information required for the user’s goals while protecting the privacy of potential suspects.

Further, there should be more legal considerations around the rights of suspects prior to convictions. If a suspect is posted to social media and then found innocent, they could have a difficult time integrating back into society; they may be rejected by the community due to biased views formed on the back of the publicly released arrest data. In line with the FIPPs principle of Data Quality and Integrity, the Baltimore City Police Department should implement a mechanism to reflect that a suspect was deemed innocent. (The Fair Information)The related posts on social media should be purged by the Police. Further, the innocent suspect’s image and personal information should be disassociated from the arrest crimes. Lastly, the Baltimore Police should consider including a column on the historical arrest data indicating whether the suspect was convicted of the crime(s) they were arrested for.

Reidentifying minority group members

We have already explored the risks of reidentification through the various methods discussed above. What we must also consider is how the release of these data sets poses a risk to different racial groups, especially minorities.

For example, people of Asian descent are present in so few numbers in the data that it poses a risk to their personal safety. Only 2% of Baltimore’s population is Asian according to 2020 census data (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020). With the rise in hate crimes against people of Asian descent throughout the United States, the re-identification possibilities with these datasets could exacerbate an already horrific situation that is unfolding in the country by informing attackers, for example, of locations or zip codes where Asian people have been arrested or been victims of a crime.

Black men are the most prominent group in the arrest records, and we were able to deduce this because of the inclusion of the race column in the arrest records. There is a tremendous risk of racial profiling as a result of the overrepresentation of minority groups such as Black men in the arrest record data. This puts already marginalized communities at risk for further trauma and can compromise the safety of the community in its entirety–something that we have seen time and time again with racial profiling taking the lives of so many people of color, specifically Black Americans.

Racial profiling has resulted in the over-policing of BIPOC bodies and neighborhoods. Because of the frankly inconceivable gravity of dangers the inclusion of race in these datasets poses, we encourage you to think about whether the utility of including this race in these datasets outweighs the risks it poses to the wellbeing and privacy of the individual members in these communities. Furthermore, for the protection of at-risk populations and minorities, as stressed above, we strongly urge that social media posts be purged as well.

Nissenbaum's framework for contextual integrity supports the inclusion of data only when there is a specific use for it. For the purposes that the government of Baltimore has given for this data being released, it is not immediately clear why race is included as a column. If one goal of making this data public is to improve transparency, then we recommend including an explanation of why each column in the data is included at such a low granularity in the first place. Thus the government can either be clear with its intention for including the column or move to remove it altogether if there is no such justifiable reason.

Due Diligence for all Datasets

In order to prevent the release of personally identifiable data or any other data that could put the privacy or personal safety of any individual at risk, it is critical that there be a standardized review process and a code of conduct that audits each data set before its release. For Open Baltimore specifically, a review process will further their goal of accountability by granting agency to the public to determine what information is available to the world. For the data itself, this might decrease its utility, but more importantly, it will accomplish a higher level of privacy and protection for the citizens of Baltimore present in the data sets. For example, a deep dive into the arrest records data via a review process may have been able to reveal the privacy harms we identified in this document and likely result in the race column ultimately not being released with the data sets. Instead, the review could have revealed that race information in aggregate could achieve the same goals or transparency without putting anyone at risk of identification.

Perturbing or sampling the data would also decrease the risk associated with releasing sensitive data sets such as the ones included in Open Baltimore. Perturbing would result in the distribution of the dataset being significantly different from the original dataset. This could help level out uneven distributions across different races, for example, and reduce the risk of racial profiling (if the race column is even included in the first place). Wilson and Rosen present simple techniques of data perturbation that Baltimore could implement with minimal effort (Wilson and Rosen 2003).

Each of the methods and mechanisms described above can be applied not only to the Open Baltimore data portal but to any publicly available dataset to decrease the risk of any sort of harm coming to those whose data is included in the data.

Conclusion

While we are concerned about the current state of the data available in the Open Baltimore dataset, we are optimistic that intentional changes and considerations could reduce our privacy concerns while still achieving the Police Department’s goal of providing citizens transparency. We believe implementing data minimization principles and defining data use specifications would greatly improve citizens’ privacy risks. We appreciate your time and look forward to hearing your feedback on this topic.
