Surveillance in Bulk

Chandler Haukap

Abstract

Every day our online footprints grow. We send emails, exchange direct messages, and store our documents in cloud servers that we will never see and never own. These effects that once existed on papers stored in filing cabinets in back offices are now highly available from anywhere in the world- from a cafe in Nepal to CIA headquarters in Virginia. With Google and Facebook holding the “keys” to our online filing cabinets we no longer have the agency to feel secure in our effects. Because the laws that govern our online privacy were written long before we understood the magnitude or usefulness of the internet, we have very few protections and therefore zero confidence that the government isn’t surveilling every text and collecting every photo. In this paper, I explore the current privacy protections in place on the internet, describe government surveillance that evades privacy protection, and raise concerns about the escalation of government surveillance. I conclude by suggesting an alternative paradigm that restores the confidence of United States citizens that their fourth amendment rights still exist in the age of information.

Overview

This work is divided into four sections. In the first section, I describe the legal mechanisms that allow the government to surveil information stored by private companies. My intent is to highlight the grey areas of the law that these mechanisms inhabit. The second section presents aggregated data from the transparency reports of some of the tech giants. The data in these transparency reports establish the magnitude of the problem. Next, I provide concrete examples of how surveillance harms individuals via misinterpretation of the data and harms the economy by throttling our ability to export our technology. Finally, I present a panel of solutions, ranging from legal changes that could put the United States on track for a more equitable internet.

Search Warrants

The Fourth Amendment of the United States Constitution describes the process that a law enforcement officer must follow in order to search or seize private property. It was drafted in response to King George’s “writs of assistance” which allowed the king’s agents to rifle through colonists’ belongings until they found something incriminating (Snyder 2007).

To obtain a warrant, a law enforcement officer must convince a judge that their search would meet three requirements: 1. Probable cause, supported by oath or affirmation. This requires that the officer have a reason to suspect that a crime is being committed. This prevents “fishing expeditions” where law enforcement searches indiscriminately until they find a crime. 2. A description of the place to be searched. Law enforcement must be clear about the scope of their search. This prevents officers from violating the privacy of entire neighborhoods. 3. The persons or things to be seized. It is insufficient to claim that a crime is being committed without understanding what that crime is and who is committing it. Simply looking “suspicious” does not give law enforcement a right to search.

Fourth Amendment in Practice

Prior to the information age, the application of the fourth amendment was fairly straightforward. In determining if the fourth amendment applied a judge need only determine if the government was trespassing on private property (Tokson 2009). When telephones were first invented they were considered communal, not personal, devices which explains the Supreme Court’s 1924 ruling that telephone calls were not protected by the fourth amendment (Olmstead v. United States 1928).

In 1976 United States v. Miller gave rise to the Third Party Doctrine. This doctrine offered fourth amendment protection exclusively to information that is not disclosed to a third party (United States v. Miller 1976) thereby stripping fourth amendment protection from phone records and, until recently, cell phone location data (Manning 2019).

The Third-Party Doctrine reigned until 2018 when cell phone geolocation was extended fourth amendment protection by the adoption of the Innescapability Doctrine (United States v. Carpenter 2018). “Innescapability” in this sense implies that cell phones are inescapable in the modern age and therefore deserve additional protections not normally ascribed to third-party information. However, the Third-Party Doctrine still applies to many pieces of sensitive information which I’ll describe below.

Geofenced Warrants

With 2.9 million downloads in 2021, Google Maps has become the de facto source of navigation information in the United States (Statista 2022). Google maps, and other navigation apps, contain volumes of location information on the majority of Americans. This information has not gone untapped by the United States government. Geofenced warrants are warrants issued to a tech company commanding that they disclose identifying information about every device that was present at a given location at a given point in time. Police then comb through this information to identify individuals that they think might have committed a crime and target them with further investigations (Harvard 2021).

The grey area that these warrants occupy is the specification of persons or things to be seized. Simply being in the public sphere is not probable cause to be searched. In fact, a 2021 ruling denying a geofenced warrant cited a Supreme Court case where police with a warrant to search a bar and bartenders illegally searched a patron of the bar (Ybarra v. Illinois 1979) arguing that because geofenced warrants do not specify the persons to be searched, are not admissible in court (Lynch and Sobel 2021).

Keyword Warrants

Google.com, the world's most popular search site, was visited 91 billion times in December 2021 alone (Similarweb 2022). Law enforcement has recently cast its gaze on this wealth of data in the form of keyword warrants. These warrants demand companies supply the names of everyone that searched for a particular keyword at a particular time (Brewser 2021). They have been issued since at least 2017 (Bhuiyan 2021) and received by tech giants including Google, Microsoft, and Yahoo (Whitaker 2022).

This type of warrant occupies a grey area between criminal intent and freedom of the press. The first amendment protects our right to obtain information, even if the government finds the information unsavory.

Genealogical Warrants

Even before human biology could be submitted to databases, the issues surrounding the privacy of our DNA have been hotly debated (Nature 2020). With the rise of DNA profiling sites like 23&Me and Ancestry.com the government has identified a new method of surveillance: genealogical warrants. The process law enforcement uses is to take a DNA sample from a crime scene, upload it to a DNA testing site, and see if there are any close relatives. Then, they interview the close relatives to see if they have any idea which member of their family the DNA belongs to (St. John 2020).

This warrant blurs the line between public and private information. One service that many people want from DNA testing sites is the ability to locate relatives. The intent of these users is to connect with relatives, not incriminate them.

FISA Warrants

The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978 established a separate court to approve warrants issued by federal agents (BJA 2022). The court is ex parte, meaning that the only parties present at the hearings are the judge and the government agents (ibid.). 11 Judges are appointed to serve in the FISA court (FISC) by the chief justice of the supreme court and these judges serve on a rolling basis (FISC 2022). The FISC approves over 99% of final warrant requests (Walton 2013).

Both foreign and domestic individuals can be targeted by FISA warrants (Lucas 2019). FISA warrants do not have to pertain to an ongoing criminal investigation. Instead, they only need to pertain to an ongoing national security investigation (BJA 2022), which implies that the information gathered might not ever be presented to an opposing attorney. This condition, coupled with the fact that most FISA warrants are issued with orders gagging the companies that receive them from disclosing to the subjects of the warrants that the government is surveilling them, results in a system that heavily favors federal surveillance over individual privacy.

In terms of fourth amendment rights, FISA warrants redefine probable cause. In a traditional warrant, the intent of the information collection is to ultimately present the information to a public court. Without the intent to pursue criminal prosecution the issuer of FISA warrants severely undermine the probable cause clause.

The rise of government surveillance

Owing to the gag orders placed on companies we do not have complete visibility into government surveillance activity. Luckily, many large companies do publish transparency reports that include government requests. These reports generally contain the same information, but owing to the lack of standardization they vary in their degree of granularity. Some sites like Google report the number of search warrants, subpoenas, and emergency requests as well as the number of requests they successfully block. Other sites like Apple seem to combine warrants and administrative subpoenas.

Why this is a problem

Below I present examples of government surveillance failing to protect our interests. This is not an exhaustive list, but it serves to exemplify the dangers of allowing surveillance to go unchallenged.

Wrong place, wrong time

At their best, geofenced warrants have allowed law enforcement to prosecute those individuals that breached the capital during the January 6, 2021 riots (Harris 2021). At their worst, they have jailed innocent people simply for being in the wrong place at the wrong time.

In 2020 Jorge Molina was arrested on suspicion of murder based on cell phone data that placed him near the scene of a murder (Harvard Law Review 2021). Police identified him as the suspect to local media and placed him in jail for six days, during which time Molina lost his job. Molina was released only after several people provided alibis, and further cell phone data revealed that the location information was incorrect (Id.). Cases like Molina reveal the fallibility of location information. No one should be placed in jail for simply walking by a crime.

DNA’s uncertainty

Because DNA is unique to an individual, most courts view it as damming evidence (Worth 2018). However, we drastically underestimate the amount of DNA we leave behind everywhere we visit. The average person sheds enough skin cells to cover a football field every 2 minutes, and we shed anywhere from 40 to 100 hairs a day (Ramirez 2020). How many times have you found a hair on yourself that did not belong to you?

A 2014 study tested the spread of DNA by asking a panel of participants to drink juice together. The researchers placed a single jug of juice between four participants and asked the participants to pour their own glasses. At the end of the session, the researchers swabbed the glasses for DNA. Not only did they identify the DNA of the individual using the glass, but they found that 33% of the participants' DNA were on glasses that they had never even touched (Goray and Oorschot 2014). This means that DNA can be transferred from a person onto an object, then from the object onto another person, then from that person to another object.

This exact scenario occurred in 2018 when Lukis Anderson was charged with murder. The only evidence against him was his DNA found on the victim. He was charged, despite hospital records proving that he was admitted for alcohol poisoning 45 minutes before the murder even took place (Worth 2018). Thanks to the work of a public defender Anderson was released. However, our blind faith in DNA evidence, coupled with the issuance of warrants to genealogy sites, could lead to the loss of freedom for countless innocent people.

The loss of a lucrative market

The executive branch’s overreach into the privacy of individuals is not only concerning to United States citizens. In recent years the European Union has begun taking actions to prevent its citizen's data from being transferred to the United States. For example, in a 2015 court case, the EU’s highest court ruled that the United State’s surveillance program “compromises the essence of the fundamental right to effective judicial protection, the existence of such a possibility being inherent in the existence of the rule of law.” (O’Brien 2015). The court again ruled against data flow from the EU and the US in 2020, citing the fact that the FISA act does not provide any protections for foreign citizens (O’Brien 2020).

While these rulings guide companies to keep European data in European nations, there is still the question of whether the US can demand that US companies with data stored in the EU disclose this information to the US government. Specifically, a German court interested in this question made an inquiry in 2021 to Texas Law professor Prof. Stephen I. Vladeck who informed them that neither the FISA act nor any other surveillance legislation protects companies from government requests when the data is not located within the United States (Vladeck 2021). This effectively means that US security agencies do not have to respect national borders.

Caitlin Fennessy of the International Association of Privacy Professionals (IAPP) identifies three potential consequences of this fracture between the EU and the US:

  1. Other data protection agencies take a similar approach in the immediate term.
  2. EU businesses back away from U.S. firms due to perceived enforcement risk.
  3. U.S. firms themselves localize services or exit the EU market.

(Fennessy 2022)

The effects of both 2 and 3 would be disastrous for the US economy. Cross-border data flows between the United States and EU are among the highest in the world, valued at over $264 billion in 2020, with the United States boasting a $22 billion dollar surplus in digital financial services (Congressional Research Service 2021). If the IAPP is correct in its analysis this trade surplus could dry up.

Europe’s rejection of American surveillance practices adds economic consequences to a practice that already violates human rights.

Touching on Beneficence

There are truly despicable crimes committed online. Every day children are exploited online (Child Advocacy Center 2022) and I suspect that public opinion would favor the perpetrators of these crimes being monitored and prosecuted with the full might of the FBI. The debate is not, and should not, center on the government’s ability to monitor online activity. To quote Justice Brandeis in his dissenting opinion on Olmstead v United States, “Experience teaches us to be most on our guard to protect liberty when the government's purposes are beneficent”.

Instead, the debate must center around how much collateral damage is acceptable in our quest for justice. As I’ve described, our current trajectory will inevitably result in innocent people like Jorge Molina being jailed. It will also severely cripple the data flow between the US and the EU which has economic consequences on both continents.

The Belmont Report sums up the problem as such: “the different claims covered by the principle of beneficence may come into conflict and force difficult choice”. We are now presented with such a choice. Below, I will present solutions in the court and in popular opinion that attempt to mitigate harm.

Solutions

Informing the Courts

Utah Law profession Matthew Tokson offers five alternatives to the inescapability clause that would better equip courts to rule on fourth amendment protections for online data. Of these five solutions, I’ve identified two that are not only legally sound but also robust from a data science perspective.

The Normative Approach

The normative approach asks judges to extend fourth amendment protection to information that an individual should feel secure in. This approach would ask judges to build a body of precedence independent from societal expectations of privacy. Tokson offers this alternative as a solution that would adapt well to new technology by encouraging judges to think critically about every new method of information surveillance.

As a data scientist, I find this approach appealing because it removed societal expectations from the equation. Even among the data-native generation, only 43% believe that they are data literate (Vigliarolo 2021), and asking societal expectations of privacy to drive the debate would mean that data privacy always lags behind new innovations. A normative approach coupled with amicus briefs by technologists would better prepare judges to handle surveillance cases than our current frameworks.

The Positive Law Regime

While the normative approach is an acceptable framework, I advocate most strongly for the positive law approach. Positive law would extend fourth amendment protection to all information that would be “illegal or tortious” for a private citizen to obtain. For example, I expect that my geolocation data cannot be accessed by my neighbor. Therefore, it should be protected by the fourth amendment.

I find this simple to implement and easily adaptable from both a legal perspective and a data science perspective. A judge would not even need to necessarily understand the technology at hand. They could simply ask the company holding the information if they would yield it up to a non-government agent willingly, and extend fourth amendment protections if the answer is “no”.

Establishing a Dialog

Whether we choose to adopt the normative approach, the positive law regime, or some other framework, we cannot hope to reign in government surveillance without allowing pluralism in the discourse. 990 FISA warrants with gag orders were issued in 2019 (Duff 2019). In order to remove the gag order, a recipient must pursue litigation against the gag order. For free speech advocates like the ACLU, this means potentially submitting 990 individual requests. With well over 90% of all FISA warrants kept secret by the FBI, how can we hope to have a democratic debate about their merits?

The solution lies in opening up the FISA courts. In 2015 free speech advocates won a small victory by permitting amici curiae to counsel FISA judges. While this is a step away from autocracy, it cannot qualify as a step toward democracy. We cannot hope to truly identify equitable solutions to government surveillance without exposing FISA rulings to the scrutiny of democracy.
